Neptune storm lifecycles and Great Dark Spot analogs

Review of the Voyager 2 Great Dark Spot, the subsequent Hubble and JWST records of comparable transient storms, and the inferred lifetime statistics for Neptune's discrete-vortex weather features.
2026-04-25 · 5 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

Neptune displays large transient anticyclonic storms that are visible against the planet's predominantly blue methane-absorption-dominated atmosphere. The original Voyager 2 Great Dark Spot (GDS-89) was the prototype of this class. Subsequent Hubble Space Telescope and JWST monitoring have documented at least five comparable features over the post-Voyager period, allowing estimation of typical storm lifetimes and locations. This report reviews the storm lifecycle and the implications for Neptune's atmospheric dynamics.

The Voyager 2 Great Dark Spot

Voyager 2 imaged the Great Dark Spot during the August 1989 flyby. The feature was located at approximately 22 degrees south latitude, occupied an area approximately 13,000 by 6,000 km (comparable to Earth's diameter), and rotated retrograde with a period of approximately 16 days. The GDS was accompanied by bright orographic methane-ice clouds that hovered above the storm and were carried along by it.

Hubble Space Telescope observations in 1994 and 1995 found that GDS-89 was no longer present. The disappearance occurred sometime between 1989 and 1994; the precise timing is not constrained because Neptune was not continuously monitored at sufficient resolution during the interval.

Subsequent storm features

Hubble has imaged at least four additional dark-spot features at high spatial resolution since 1996. Each was anticyclonic, located in the southern hemisphere, persistent over multiple imaging epochs, and accompanied by orographic clouds. The most-studied features are NDS-1994 (the southern-equatorial spot first detected in 1994), NDS-2015, and NDS-2018. Each persisted for several years before fading from monitoring observations. Two of these features were identified mid-life and observed for approximately three to five years; the others were identified late and observed for less than one year.

JWST observations beginning in 2023 have provided the highest-resolution thermal-infrared images of Neptune storms, resolving the temperature structure within and around dark spots. The JWST data show pronounced asymmetric warm and cool regions around storm cores that match circulation predictions from giant-planet vortex theory.

Lifetime statistics

The combined Voyager-Hubble-JWST record provides limited but informative storm-lifetime statistics. The post-Voyager monitoring spans approximately 35 years and covers most of one Neptune year (164.8 Earth years). Within this baseline, at least five Great-Dark-Spot-class features have been documented. Average lifetime, where it can be estimated, is two to six years. Storms appear to form preferentially at southern mid-latitudes, with no documented features at high northern latitudes during the modern record.

The total fraction of time during which a Great-Dark-Spot-class feature is present somewhere on Neptune is high - estimated at greater than 50 percent based on the monitoring record. The instantaneous count is typically zero or one; two simultaneous features have not been confirmed.

Comparison with Jupiter and Saturn vortices

Neptune's dark spots differ from Jupiter's Great Red Spot and Saturn's polar hexagon in several respects. The most striking is lifetime: Jupiter's GRS has persisted for at least 350 years and shows no sign of imminent dissipation, while Neptune's largest documented storms last only several years. The contrast is interpreted as a consequence of the relative strength of the underlying zonal-flow shear: Jupiter's strong differential rotation provides sustained vorticity input that maintains the GRS, while Neptune's weaker shear allows discrete vortices to dissipate within a few advective timescales.

The orographic methane-ice clouds above the dark spots are a Neptune-specific feature without counterparts at Jupiter or Saturn. The clouds form because the rising air around the storm centre cools through methane condensation. The clouds are themselves transient, sometimes persisting after the underlying dark spot has faded from optical visibility.

Dynamical interpretation

The discrete-vortex interpretation of Neptune's dark spots is supported by general-circulation modelling that produces stable anticyclonic features at southern mid-latitudes given Neptune's measured zonal-wind profile. The features are interpreted as Rossby-wave-trapped vortices in a three-dimensional atmosphere with potential-vorticity gradients consistent with Neptune's wind structure.

Outlook

No Neptune mission is currently in development. The 2023 Decadal Survey identified Uranus orbital exploration as the higher priority outer-ice-giant target. Continued Hubble and JWST monitoring at decadal-scale cadence remains the only Neptune storm-tracking data source. The Vera C. Rubin Observatory will provide additional high-cadence photometric monitoring of Neptune at full operations.
